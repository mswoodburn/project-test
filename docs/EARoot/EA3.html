The collection object concept is central to the CMS data model. This broadly represents a physical object that is part of, or is being brought into, the Museum’s collection and/or custody, and is subject to the many processes involved in collections management and cataloguing.<br/>Information will be attached to a collection object to reflect where, when and by whom it was collected, who is responsible for its care within the collection, where it is (and has been) stored within the Museum, and what its scientific classification and stratigraphic origin is. In addition to this, there is a whole extra wealth of detailed data that is not represented at the level of detail in the conceptual model.<br/>The most significant modelling challenge however is that in the real world, a collection object is not a simple definition. While some examples are relatively straightforward, such as an individual insect mounted on a pin, many examples are more complex. Examples include:<br/><ul>
<li>a number of different organisms stored and handled together, such as a jar of frogs</li><li>a single organism with parts that have been separated and are stored in different locations, such as a skeleton made up of multiple bones</li><li>an unsorted lot of material that may belong to a number of organisms, such as the contents of an insect trap</li><li>a single physical object that may contain a number of different components that are classified independently, such as a rock slab containing multiple fossilised organisms</li><li>an object which has produced derivative objects, such as an insect which has had a leg removed for analysis</li><li>an object which has had samples taken for DNA analysis, such as a section taken from a dried leaf on a herbarium sheet</li><li>an object which represents organisms from multiple disciplines, such as a gall (entomology) on a leaf (botany)</li><li>two objects that may have been collected together and represent a real-world relationship, such as a host-parasite relationship represented by a louse that was found on a bird specimen</li></ul>
The impact of this is that:<br/><ol>
<li>a ‘collection object’ in the data will not always represent just a single object in the physical world.</li><li>there are a range of complex relationships that need to be maintained between objects, groups of objects, and their various components, derivatives and samples</li><li>each of these examples may be subject together or independently to the various collections management and cataloguing processes</li><li>relationships must be maintained and updated over time to retain provenance information, as processes cause objects to be created, split, merged and destroyed</li><li>the need to apportion scientific classifications to these adds complexity – for example, a curator or researcher needs to know whether multiple objects (e.g. bones or DNA samples) originate from the same individual of a particular species</li></ol>
For these reasons, the collection object cannot be represented by just a single simple entity in the data model. <br/>
